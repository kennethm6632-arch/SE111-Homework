Review

Kenneth April 2025

The Mask is one of those rare films where the lead actor and the material were made for each other. Jim Carrey was already known for his elastic humor but Stanley Ipkiss transforming into the loud, chaotic, cartoon version of himself is one of the most purely entertaining performances of the 1990s and the special effects, which still hold up surprisingly well, give Carrey's energy the visual language it deserved.

When my dad introduced me to this movie I thought it was going to be a boring old 90s movie, but it ended up becoming one of my all time favorites. The humor, the performance from Jim Carrey all tied in to make this movie so memorable.

Revisiting The Mask today, what stands out most is how much fun everyone on screen is clearly having. That energy is contagious a classic of '90s comedy.

Trivia

  • Jim Carrey was paid $450,000 for the role a relatively modest sum compared to later films because he had not yet broken out as a major star before its release.
  • The Mask was Cameron Diaz's feature film debut. She beat out over 1,500 other actresses who auditioned for the role of Tina Carlyle.
  • The film's visual effects team used a then-cutting-edge technique called "performance capture" combined with traditional CGI to achieve Carrey's rubber-face transformations.
  • The cartoon-like physics and exaggerated gags were directly inspired by classic Tex Avery and Warner Bros. animation, which the filmmakers referenced heavily during production.
